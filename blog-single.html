Investigating the association between Socioeconomic status and No2 

It has been shown that air pollution is related to several health risks, such as stroke and aggravated respiratory disease. World Health Organization reported that 4.2 million deaths every year are caused by exposure to outdoor air pollution.According to Hajat et al. (2015), ”Most North American studies have shown that areas where low socioeconomic status (SES) communities dwell experience higher concentrations of criteria air pollutants”.

The scientific interest of this project is to learn about differential levels of NO2 exposure for different population groups in Alameda County. We connected Aclima’s hyperlocal air quality data to neighborhood-level (block group-level) measures of demographic factors including race/ethnicity, age, income and education, employment status from American Community Survey (ACS) data, created data visualization and developed spatial and non-spatial models for statistical inference.

DatASETS  

We used American Community Survey(ACS) data to extract Socioeconomic information by block groups(the finest level of demographics we can access). The SES information was mapped to the air pollutants data provided by Aclima, Inc. We extracted educational attainment, income, unemployment, race/ethnicity and age variables from ACS dataset.

Exploratory Analysis  

We first plotted the distribution of each demographic, and then compared them to the distribution of NO2 to see whether there were similarities.

Methodology  

Our primary goal is to examine the association between SES variables and NO2 air pollutant level. Linear regression models were built with log-transformed NO2 as outcome, with socio-economic related variables, income, education and employment status, adjusted for race/ethnicity and age.Correlation structure was added to the linear main model, adjusting for spatial correlation between NO2 measurements. The correlation model parameters is nuisance parameters.

Two modeling methods were used for our analysis, Generalized estimating equation (GEE) and Gaussian Bayesian spatial regression models using Nearest Neighbor Gaussian Processes (NNGP). For data less than limit of detection, we substituted them by LOD/sqrt2 based on the guidance of Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).
